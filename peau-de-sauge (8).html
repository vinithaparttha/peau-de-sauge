The season, first

This is a formula about a season, not two plants

Before it's about clary sage or lavender, Peau de Sauge is about what Mediterranean summer actually feels like at close range: the flat white heat of early afternoon, cicadas going constantly from the treeline, dust on bare ankles, stone walls too hot to lean on, and the particular quiet of a village at 3pm when everyone is inside. Lavender and clary sage happen to be the plants that grow best in exactly that stress — they're a symptom of the season, not the subject of it.

That's the ordering the whole formula follows: heat first, plants second. The citrus and green top notes read like shade and shadow against the glare; the herbal heart is the scrubland itself, sun-hit and slightly bitter; the amber-musk base is the stone and skin that hold the day's heat long after the light has gone.

The name

What "Peau de Sauge" means

Peau de sauge translates plainly as "sage skin." In French perfumery, peau describes a composition built to behave like skin rather than a cloud — quiet, warm, diffusing close to the body instead of projecting outward. Sauge is clary sage, Salvia sclarea, the plant that gives the formula its dry, tea-like, faintly smoky warmth.

The name doesn't describe a scent sprayed onto skin. It describes the skin itself, after a day spent among the sage rows — sun, crushed herb oil, and the heat of the afternoon, held in the fabric of a shirt collar by evening.

The brief

Built for the dry heat, not despite it

The lavender and clary sage are grown on the Haute-Provence and Vaucluse plateaus — Valensole and the Luberon at 350–700m, the higher Sault plateau at 800–900m where the finest lavender grows. It's a Mediterranean climate in the strict sense: hot, dry summers with almost no rain from June through August, and the Mistral, a strong dry wind, keeps humidity low year-round. Heat-and-drought stress is exactly what concentrates essential oil in these plants — the hotter and drier the summer, the stronger the material a distiller gets from it.

Harvest follows that heat rather than a fixed date: lower fields are typically cut from mid-July, higher ones not until mid-August, timing set by oil content, not the calendar. That's the season Peau de Sauge is built around — cicadas, dust, cut stalks drying in the sun before distillation, the particular quiet of a Provençal afternoon at 32°C. The hay note is true Hay Absolute, not a synthetic accord, made from the same cut-and-dried grass smell that fills the air across the plateau in July.
